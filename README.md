# CS230-Portfolio
Reflection
The Gaming Room was the client for this project, and their goal was to expand their existing Android game, Draw It or Lose It, into a scalable, web‑based, multi‑platform application. They needed a design that supported multiple teams, multiple players per team, unique naming across all entities, and a single authoritative game service to manage state. My task was to create a complete software design document that translated these requirements into a clear, maintainable, and technically sound architecture.

One area I feel I did particularly well was clearly communicating the system’s structure and constraints. The sections on requirements, design constraints, and the domain model were especially strong because they broke down complex ideas into organized, understandable components. I also made sure the document was written in a way that both technical team members and non‑technical stakeholders could follow, which is essential in real‑world software design.

Working through the design document before writing any code was extremely helpful. Defining the classes, relationships, and patterns early on made the development process more predictable and reduced the risk of rework. The UML‑based domain model and the emphasis on object‑oriented principles gave me a blueprint that guided the implementation and ensured consistency with the client’s needs.

If I were to revise one part of the document, I would expand the evaluation section with more detailed comparisons of performance, scalability, and long‑term maintenance across platforms. While the analysis was solid, adding more quantitative or scenario‑based justification would strengthen the recommendations and provide even clearer guidance for the client.

Interpreting the user’s needs required focusing on the gameplay experience they wanted to preserve—fast, synchronized guessing rounds with multiple teams and players. I translated those needs into technical requirements such as concurrency control, unique identifiers, and centralized state management. Considering user needs is essential because software design must align with how real people interact with the system. Ignoring user expectations leads to usability issues, inefficiency, and costly redesigns later.

My approach to designing the software centered on analyzing requirements, identifying constraints, and modeling the system before coding. I relied on UML diagrams, object‑oriented design principles, and architectural evaluation to guide decisions. In future projects, I would continue using these strategies while incorporating more iterative design reviews and early prototyping to validate assumptions sooner. This combination of structured planning and iterative refinement supports both clarity and adaptability when designing complex systems.
